Short Story

Sarah’s day began like every other: already behind. By 6:45 a.m, I started hearing dishes clanging through the window. She was packing two lunches, one with no dairy and the other with no crust. The dishwasher hadn’t been run the night before. Again. A small mountain of plates and utensils waited in silence. She rinsed a spoon under cold water and stirred almond butter into her daughter’s oatmeal, checking her phone with her free hand. Three unread work emails. One calendar alert for a team call at nine. A reminder to send a field trip form. David shuffled into the kitchen at 7, still rubbing his eyes. “Want coffee?” she asked without looking up. “I’ll get it in a sec,” he said. Then added, “By the way, I folded that basket of laundry last night. Thought I’d get ahead of it.” She nodded, her mouth full of a sigh she didn’t have time to let out. By 7:40, both kids got dressed and halfway through arguing over who got to ride in the front seat on Friday, their dad’s drop-off day. Sarah adjusted her blouse in the hallway mirror. A faint oatmeal smear marked her shoulder. She changed. The house was humming, barely held together. Everything got done, not perfectly, but done. And still, it felt like nothing ever stayed fixed.
